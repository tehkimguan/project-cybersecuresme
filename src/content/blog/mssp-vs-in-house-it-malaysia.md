**For most Malaysian SMEs with fewer than 100 staff, a managed security service provider (MSSP) will deliver better security coverage at lower cost than a fully in-house team.** That said, the right answer depends on your industry, the sensitivity of the data you hold, and what your internal team is already capable of. This article lays out the real tradeoffs so you can make an informed decision.

## What each model actually means

### In-house IT security

Building your security capability internally means hiring one or more staff members whose job includes managing security tools, responding to alerts, patching systems, and advising the business on security decisions. In larger organisations this is a dedicated security team. In smaller businesses it is often an IT generalist who also handles hardware, user support, and network administration alongside security.

The key constraint is that a single in-house person cannot provide 24-hour monitoring, maintain expertise across a rapidly changing threat landscape, and still handle the day-to-day IT work a growing business needs. The moment that person is sick, on leave, or leaves the company, coverage drops significantly.

### Managed Security Service Provider (MSSP)

An MSSP is a company that delivers security services to multiple clients, typically under a monthly subscription. Depending on the package, they may provide:

- 24/7 monitoring of your endpoints and network for suspicious activity
- Automated patching and updates
- Incident detection and response
- A helpdesk for security queries
- Periodic reporting on your security posture
- Virtual CISO (vCISO) services for strategic advice

Because they serve many clients, MSSPs spread the cost of expensive tooling and specialised staff across their client base. A small business can access capabilities that would cost far more to replicate internally.

Under the Cyber Security Act 2024 (Act 854), administered by the National Cyber Security Agency (NACSA), providers of certain managed security services are required to hold a NACSA licence. Verifying licence status before engaging an MSSP is now part of standard due diligence for Malaysian businesses.

## Comparing the two models

| Factor | In-house | MSSP |
|---|---|---|
| Coverage hours | Business hours unless overtime is paid | 24/7 in most packages |
| Specialist depth | Generalist with some security knowledge | Specialists across multiple domains |
| Cost predictability | Variable (salary, tools, training) | Fixed monthly fee |
| Continuity risk | High if one person holds most knowledge | Low; shared across a team |
| Compliance familiarity | Depends on the individual hired | Should include PDPA and Act 854 context |
| Time to deploy | Weeks to months to hire and onboard | Days to weeks |

## When in-house makes sense

There are scenarios where building internal capability is the right call:

- **Regulated sectors with strict data residency rules.** Some financial services businesses regulated under Bank Negara Malaysia's Risk Management in Technology (RMiT) framework have specific requirements about who can access security data and where it is stored. In these cases, a hybrid approach (internal team plus MSSP for monitoring) is common.
- **Organisations with very high data sensitivity.** If your business handles proprietary information that must not leave a controlled environment, full outsourcing may not be appropriate.
- **Businesses large enough to justify a dedicated team.** Once you reach 200-plus staff with a meaningful IT estate, the economics can shift. At that scale, building internal capability with external specialist support for niche areas (penetration testing, incident response) often makes sense.

## When an MSSP makes sense

For the typical Malaysian SME, outsourcing is the better choice in most of these situations:

- You have an IT generalist who handles everything from printer problems to network setup and cannot realistically add security monitoring to that workload.
- You want after-hours or weekend coverage without paying for standby staff.
- A customer or regulator is asking for evidence of your security posture and you need to get to a defensible standard quickly.
- You have experienced an incident and want ongoing monitoring to prevent a repeat.
- Your personal data processing under the PDPA 2010 (Act 709) creates breach notification obligations and you want a provider who understands the reporting timelines.

## The hybrid option

Many Malaysian SMEs land on a hybrid model: one internal IT person who owns relationships, handles day-to-day requests, and makes decisions, supported by an MSSP for monitoring, patching, and specialist response. This gives you a business-facing point of contact internally and 24/7 coverage externally, without the cost of a full internal security function.

## Questions to ask before deciding

Whichever direction you lean, these questions help clarify what you actually need:

- What are your biggest risks right now - is it ransomware, phishing, regulatory compliance, or something else?
- What hours do you need coverage? Are your systems accessed outside business hours?
- What does an incident cost your business per hour of downtime? (The [IT downtime cost calculator](/tools/it-downtime-cost-calculator) can help you put a number on this.)
- What is your realistic monthly budget for security? (The [cybersecurity budget estimator](/tools/security-budget-estimator) gives an indicative range based on your size and sector.)
- Do you have any regulatory requirements that constrain who can access your security data?
