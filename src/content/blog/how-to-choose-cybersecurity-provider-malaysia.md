**The short answer: check the licence, define the scope, and test their support before you sign.** Choosing a cybersecurity provider is one of the highest-impact procurement decisions a small business can make. A good fit improves your security posture and frees your team to focus on the business. A poor one drains budget without measurably reducing risk. This checklist helps you evaluate providers against criteria that matter for a Malaysian SME.

## Why this decision is harder than it used to be

The cybersecurity market in Malaysia has grown substantially. That is mostly good news, as more competition has brought more SME-focused offerings. But it also means more variation in quality, scope, and pricing. The Cyber Security Act 2024 (Act 854), administered by the National Cyber Security Agency (NACSA), adds a regulatory dimension: providers of certain cybersecurity services commercially are now required to hold a NACSA licence. Engaging an unlicensed provider for regulated services creates risk for your business, not just theirs.

## The checklist

### 1. Verify NACSA licensing for regulated services

Ask any prospective provider directly: "Which NACSA licence categories do you hold?" Then verify the claim on the NACSA website or by contacting NACSA. This applies particularly to providers offering managed security operations, penetration testing, or computer security incident response as a commercial service. A reputable provider will volunteer this information without prompting.

### 2. Clarify exactly what is included

Security services are frequently mis-sold on scope. Before signing, get written answers to these questions:

- What assets are covered (laptops, servers, mobile devices, cloud services, OT equipment)?
- What does monitoring cover, and what hours is the team staffed?
- If an incident occurs, who responds, and within what time?
- Are software licences included in the monthly fee or billed separately?
- What does offboarding look like if you switch providers?

If the provider cannot answer these questions clearly, that is itself an answer.

### 3. Check whether they have experience with businesses your size

Providers who primarily serve large enterprises often apply the same processes to smaller clients, at a price point and complexity level that does not fit. Ask for two or three reference customers of similar staff size and sector. A good managed security provider should be able to point you to clients in manufacturing, retail, professional services, or whichever sector matches yours.

### 4. Assess their alignment with Malaysian compliance requirements

A provider operating in Malaysia should be familiar with:

- The Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA, Act 709) and its 2024 amendments, including breach notification timelines
- The Cyber Security Act 2024 reporting obligations for NCII operators
- Bank Negara Malaysia's Risk Management in Technology (RMiT) policy, if your business operates in or serves the financial sector

If you ask a provider how their services help you meet PDPA obligations and they give a vague answer, look elsewhere.

### 5. Understand the pricing model and what triggers extra charges

Common models include per-device monthly fees, per-user fees, and tiered packages. Ask specifically what triggers an out-of-scope charge. Some providers include incident response in the monthly fee up to a defined number of hours; others bill separately from the first minute. Knowing this before an incident is considerably better than finding out during one.

### 6. Evaluate responsiveness before you commit

Send a detailed enquiry by email and note how long it takes to receive a substantive reply. Ask a specific technical question during the sales conversation. If the sales team cannot answer technical questions or consistently escalates without follow-up, that is a signal about how support will work post-contract.

### 7. Read the contract before signing

Contracts for security services sometimes contain clauses that limit liability in ways that are difficult to enforce or that give the provider broad access rights without corresponding obligations. Have someone read it who understands technology contracts. Pay particular attention to:

- Data handling and subprocessor arrangements (relevant to PDPA compliance)
- Termination notice periods and data return obligations
- What the provider's liability is in the event of a breach during their watch

## Matching provider type to your needs

Different categories of provider suit different situations:

| Situation | Likely provider type |
|---|---|
| You want ongoing monitoring and a helpdesk | Managed Security Service Provider (MSSP) |
| A customer or regulator is asking for evidence of your security posture | GRC consultant, auditor |
| You need to test whether your website or network can be attacked | Penetration testing firm |
| Your staff keep falling for phishing tests or clicking bad links | Security awareness training provider |
| You suffered a breach and need immediate help | Incident response specialist |

## One more check: ask how they handle your data

Your security provider will have access to sensitive information about your systems. Ask where that data is stored, who can access it within their organisation, and what happens to it when the contract ends. Under the PDPA 2010, your business remains responsible for personal data you collect, even when it is processed by a third party on your behalf. A provider that cannot clearly articulate their own data handling practices is unlikely to help you meet yours.
